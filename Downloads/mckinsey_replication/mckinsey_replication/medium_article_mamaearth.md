# Rebuilding a Consumer Supply Chain Diagnostic with Public Data

In this project, I used a structured consulting-style diagnostic to study supply chain opportunities for a leading Indian D2C brand portfolio, using publicly available information.

Most writeups either stay very technical or very generic. I aimed for a practical middle ground: identify where value is likely hidden, what can be estimated with confidence, and what still needs first-party operational data.

## What I Assessed
I reviewed five operational levers that typically drive supply chain performance:
1. Demand sensing
2. Supply visibility
3. Inventory optimization
4. Logistics optimization
5. Digital-commerce integration

## What the Analysis Found
- Some levers can be assessed reasonably well with public data and proxy signals.
- Other levers, especially logistics and real-time visibility, require deeper operational datasets to model accurately.
- The strongest insight was not a single number; it was where confidence is high versus where assumptions are still weak.

## Why This Matters
For management teams, the key question is not “Can we model everything perfectly today?”  
It is: “Where should we act first, and where should we invest in better data before acting?”

This framework helps separate:
- **Immediate action areas** (higher confidence)
- **Pilot-test areas** (medium confidence)
- **Data-priority areas** (low confidence)

## What I Changed After Expert Feedback
After practitioner feedback, I tightened assumptions and presentation quality:
- Removed any wording that implied proxy data equals proprietary transaction data.
- Added explicit confidence and assumption labeling.
- Shifted communication format toward management-facing narrative distribution.

## Closing
This project is a practical exercise in structured problem-solving under real-world data constraints: start with transparent assumptions, test them with experts, and iterate toward stronger operating decisions.
